Case Study · Spring 2026

CORE Intelligence.

I built an AI intelligence pipeline that turned hours of manual research into a five-minute daily briefing for Compass Group leadership.

While at Compass Group’s CORE Solutions division, I noticed our leadership team was spending too much time sorting through industry news from dozens of different sources. I built an AI-powered intelligence pipeline that automatically filtered more than 10 RSS feeds against our strategic priorities and delivered only the most relevant technology updates directly into Microsoft Teams. What used to take hours each week became a five-minute daily review.

From manual scan to automated signal.

Six phases from problem definition to a working system used by executive leadership.

01
Phase 01
Understanding the team, the mission, and the problem.
+

CORE Solutions was Compass Group’s internal innovation team. We focused on improving operations across supply chain, sustainability, food service, and enterprise technology by identifying better ways to solve problems before they became business challenges.

Part of that work meant staying ahead of where technology was going. New AI tools, automation platforms, startups, and industry trends were emerging every week, and leadership needed to know what was actually worth paying attention to.

The problem wasn’t a lack of information—it was too much of it.

Executives were spending hours every week reading industry publications, vendor announcements, newsletters, and trade articles trying to separate meaningful innovations from marketing noise. It was valuable work, but it took time away from strategy and execution.

02
Phase 02
The approach — build small, use what we already have.
+

Instead of proposing a vendor tool, I designed a lightweight in-house pipeline using Microsoft ecosystem tools already licensed at Compass. No procurement cycle. No new contracts. No consultants.

The design principle was narrow: eliminate the manual filtering step entirely. Not augment it. Not summarize it. Remove it. Executives would see only articles pre-scored as relevant to CORE Solutions' strategic priorities, along with a one-line rationale explaining why each article surfaced.

The smaller the surface, the faster it could ship — and the faster it shipped, the sooner leadership would get their time back.

03
Phase 03
The architecture — three layers, all in-house.
+

I built the system in three layers, each mapped to a specific tool already available in the Compass Microsoft environment:

  • Ingestion — Python scripts. Custom parsers pulled from 10+ RSS feeds covering supply chain technology, foodservice operations, AI-in-enterprise, and adjacent industry news. Feeds were normalized into a common structured format.
  • Classification — Power Automate + AI Builder. Each article ran through Microsoft's AI classification against a defined rubric of Compass's strategic priorities. Articles scored above threshold moved forward; the rest were filtered out.
  • Delivery — Microsoft Teams. Curated articles posted to a leadership Teams channel with a short classification rationale. Executives could scan the day's signal in one glance, click through for detail only when needed.

The whole system ran on tools Compass already owned. Zero incremental cost, zero external dependencies, full IT compliance out of the box.

04
Phase 04
The build — a few weeks, one person.
+

I built end-to-end alone — writing the Python parsers, wiring the Power Automate flows, tuning the AI classification prompts, and setting up the Teams delivery channel. No engineering team. No project manager. No consultants.

The bulk of the work went into the classification rubric, not the plumbing. The plumbing was straightforward. The hard part was defining what counted as "strategically relevant" precisely enough that an AI classifier could apply the definition consistently across articles from very different sources — a supply chain trade journal reads nothing like a general AI news feed.

Total build time from concept to a system leadership was actively using: a few weeks.

05
Phase 05
The rollout — refine on real feedback.
+

Rolled out first to the CORE Solutions leadership team — 3–4 executives who had been doing the manual scan themselves. They became both the users and the source of feedback.

The first two weeks were tuning. Some strategic categories came back too noisy — the classifier was surfacing too many marginal articles. Others were too narrow — missing signal the executives cared about. I refined the classification rubric based on their weekly feedback, watching what they read versus what they skipped.

By the end of the second iteration, the leadership team was checking the Teams channel instead of scanning publications directly. The behavior had shifted.

06
Phase 06
The outcome — decisions moved faster.
+

The measurable win was time: 5–10 hours of executive review time saved per week across the leadership team. Executives could scan the day's signal in under five minutes instead of the 45–60 minutes it had been costing them.

But the more important shift was in the meetings. Emerging-technology conversations moved from "what's out there?" to "how do we act on this?" The filtering step no longer belonged to the executives. Their time went to the decisions.

The system also set an internal pattern: AI tooling as small, sharp, in-house solutions — not vendor procurement projects. A model I would have continued expanding across CORE Solutions had the division not been restructured shortly after.

What the system delivered

Small system, disproportionate value.

  • 5–10 hours per week of executive review time returned to strategic work and decisions.
  • 3–4 executives actively using the system as their primary intake for emerging-technology signal.
  • Zero incremental spend — built entirely on Microsoft tools Compass already licensed.
  • Shifted leadership conversations from "what's out there" to "how do we act on this."
  • Established a template for AI-native tooling: small, sharp, in-house, built to remove a specific friction.
The best AI tools aren't the biggest. They're the ones that remove a specific piece of friction from a specific person's day.
