At Cardinal Health I led the WaveMark platform lifecycle for six health systems, including Johns Hopkins and Cleveland Clinic, deploying RFID and Kanban inventory tracking into 500+ procedural and nursing areas, and owning the customer journey from sales scoping through go-live and beyond.

The Work

Six phases. Each one earned the next.

01
Pre-Sale Discovery & Scoping
~4–6 weeks

Before any implementation begins, the question is whether it's even the right fit. I partnered with the Cardinal Health sales team during pre-sale discovery. The goal was to surface the technical and operational realities that would shape or sometimes kill the rollout before contracts were signed.

Key activities
  • Site visits to procedural and nursing areas
  • Identifying clinical, supply chain, and IT stakeholders
  • Assessing existing inventory and ERP infrastructure
  • Setting accurate expectations with health system leadership
  • Scoping feasibility for RFID and Kanban deployment
02
Requirements & Workflow Mapping
~6–8 weeks

Inventory in a procedural area at Johns Hopkins doesn't look like inventory in a nursing unit at Cleveland Clinic; the workflows, the case carts, the par levels, the people involved are all different. I led multi-stakeholder discovery to map the actual flow of supplies, identify friction points, and define what success would look like in each environment.

Key activities
  • Process mapping across procedural and nursing areas
  • Stakeholder interviews with supply chain, OR/nursing leadership, IT, finance
  • Defining KPIs and customer success criteria
  • Documenting current-state vs. future-state workflows
  • Identifying integration requirements with existing ERP and EHR systems
03
System Configuration
~8–10 weeks

WaveMark out of the box isn't WaveMark at Johns Hopkins. The platform had to be configured to each health system's catalogue, par levels, departmental hierarchy, and reporting needs. I owned this configuration work by translating the workflow maps into platform settings that actually reflected how supplies moved on the floor.

Key activities
  • Platform configuration to health system catalogue and par structure
  • RFID hardware and tag specification
  • Kanban bin configuration and replenishment logic
  • Integration with existing ERP, EHR, and inventory systems
  • User access roles and permissions
  • User acceptance testing with health system designees
04
Training & Enablement
~4–6 weeks

A SaaS platform is only as good as the people who actually use it. With 500+ procedural and nursing areas across the customer base, training had to scale not just one-time sessions, but train-the-trainer programs that would survive turnover and shift changes.

Key activities
  • Role-based training for supply chain, clinical, and management users
  • Train-the-trainer programs with health system designees
  • Standard operating procedures and quick-reference guides
  • Hands-on workflow simulations before go-live
  • Feedback loops to surface confusion before it hit production
05
Go-Live
~2–4 weeks

Go-live is the moment everything is real. I served as the primary technical escalation point during go-live and stabilization, coordinating across clinical, supply chain, and engineering teams to resolve issues in near-real time and protect the customer experience when the stakes were highest.

Key activities
  • Phased rollout by department or service line
  • On-site and remote command-center support during cutover
  • Real-time issue triage across clinical, supply chain, and engineering
  • Daily standups with health system leadership
  • Continuous communication on issue status and resolution
06
Hypercare & Measured Outcomes
8–12 weeks

The story doesn't end at go-live. Hypercare is the period when adoption either sticks or doesn't; when the team is still close enough to catch problems but the customer is starting to own the system. I owned the hypercare program structure, the KPIs, and the eventual measurement that proved the implementation worked.

Key activities
  • Daily → weekly → monthly cadence of customer success reviews
  • KPI tracking: inventory accuracy, expiration rates, staff time
  • Continuous improvement on workflows and configurations
  • Final outcome documentation and case-study build-out
  • Handoff to long-term customer success
Measured Outcomes

What the work produced.

Documented results across the deployed customer base, the kind of numbers that hold up under audit.

  • $2.6M+ in documented cost savings across the customer base
  • 50% reduction in expired inventory across six health systems
  • Six health systems onboarded, including Johns Hopkins and Cleveland Clinic
  • 500+ procedural and nursing areas deployed with RFID and Kanban inventory tracking
  • Promotion from SaaS Supply Chain Analyst to Senior SaaS Implementation Specialist after six months
